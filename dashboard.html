Project Summary

This dashboard explores two research questions we are interested in learning more about centered around political systems and economic outcomes:

  1. Female Leadership & GDP — How has Gross Domestic Product (GDP) changed over time for the countries with the most years of female national leadership?

  2. Regime Type & Unemployment — Does the type of political regime a country operates under affect its unemployment rate?

The analysis draws on three datasets covering 192 sovereign countries from 1950–2020, combining geopolitical data with GDP and unemployment figures.

Political System Data
Bjørnskov–Rode Dataset

Our first dataset was created by two economics researchers who were studying different governments and how they change over time. The purpose of this dataset was to be a resource for those interested in studying how different countries and political systems transition through the years. Additionally, the two researchers, Christian Bjørnskov and Martin Rode, when doing their own research, felt that the data available at the time was inadequate.

The dataset includes information on 192 different sovereign countries and 16 other self-governed territories from the years 1950 to 2020 on each of these countries’ regime type, democratic status, leadership, elections, transfers of power, and their colonial history.

This dataset was an expansion on other collections of geopolitical data that the authors took and added more information to. The information included features of government, elections, leadership types, and more. When it came to our analysis for this data, it was incredibly helpful to have all this information because it gave us the ability to have an accurate list of all the sovereign countries and classify them into different categories based on regime, leader, and more. Additionally, it provided us with additional information that gave context to each of these countries.

Gross Domestic Product Data
API Ninjas GDP API

Our second dataset comes from API Ninjas and is an API with GDP data on all 192 sovereign countries. This data provided us with a wide range of GDP-related variables and information for each country. Some examples of this include GDP per country, GDP per country per capita, nominal GDP, and more. The observational unit was each country and year combination, the API provides current and historical GDP data for countries around the world. API Ninjas didn’t explicitly state where they got their data from, so we don’t have full transparency on where the data came from, nor why they collected it.

These data are of interest to our first research question because GDP is one of the most used and recognized measures of national economic output. By linking GDP figures to the combined dataset, we are able to track how the economies of countries with more frequent female leadership have changed over time and see whether any patterns or relationships exist between leadership and economic performance. For our research purposes, we focused on GDP per country in billions of United States dollars. Additionally, when extracting this data from the API to merge it with our geopolitical data, we used both the country name and code.

Note: API Ninjas does not explicitly disclose its primary data sources.
Unemployment Data
API Ninjas Unemployment API

Our third dataset is from API Ninjas and is an API with unemployment data. This API provides data on the historical, current, and projected future unemployment statistics about every country in the world. This data can be retrieved through programming and can be queried by country name or ISO country code. The response variables in the data includes the country, the year of observation, and the unemployment rate in percentage form. The observational unit was each country and year combination. API Ninjas didn’t explicitly state where they got their data from, so we don’t have full transparency on where the data came from. The data was collected over multiple years and over a lot of countries to get the most in depth and detailed and informative data possible.

A country’s unemployment rate is important because it shows how healthy its economy is, with low unemployment usually meaning people are working, earning money, and spending, which helps the economy grow, and high unemployment suggesting economic problems, with fewer jobs available and lower incomes. Different organizations like governments and businesses use this information on unemployment to make decisions about policies, hiring, and investments. By combining this unemployment dataset with our original one, we are able to answer questions regarding the relationship between a country’s unemployment rate and their government or socio-economic characteristics, in this case whether the regime of the country affects the unemployment rate.

In our first table, we show the countries with the highest number of years of female national leadership along with their average GDP. Denmark and the United Kingdom have the greatest number of years (41), followed by the Netherlands and Ireland, while other countries have fewer years of female leadership. The average GDP values vary widely across these countries, ranging from very low values like for Aruba and Liberia, to much higher values like for the United Kingdom), indicating substantial differences in economic size among them.

In this table, we compared GDP at the first and last available years of data for each country, along with the total and percent change over time. All countries show an increase in GDP, though the magnitude of growth varies widely across countries. Some countries, such as Ireland and Hong Kong, have very large percent increases, while others like Aruba and Liberia show more modest growth over their observed time periods.

The results of the linear regression model on the relationship between female leadership and GDP show that there is no statistically significant association between the two variables. We decided to use GDP logged to reduce skewness that we saw in our original analysis through plotting. The model produced an F-statistic of 0.664 with a p-value of 0.416. Because this p-value is greater than 0.05, we fail to reject the null hypothesis and conclude that there is no evidence that countries with female leaders have different GDP levels compared to those without female leaders.

Although the estimated coefficient for female leadership is positive, and 5.38 for normal log which suggests higher GDP in countries with female leaders by over 500%, this result is not statistically significant and should be interpreted with caution. The large standard error & R squared indicates that the estimate varies greatly, and the model itself explains very little of the variation in GDP (R² = 0.002). This means that female leadership alone is not a meaningful predictor of economic GDP in this analysis. Additionally, a substantial number of observations were dropped due to missing data in both gender leadership and GDP, which may further limit the reliability and generalizability of these results.

Female Leadership & GDP

Overall, the analysis of the top countries with the most years of female national leadership shows that GDP increased over time for all of these countries, although the magnitude of growth varied widely. The tables and visualizations for these top countries indicate that similar lengths of female leadership are associated with very different GDP levels and growth patterns, suggesting no clear relationship within this subset. When expanding the analysis to all countries using the regression model, there is also no statistically significant association between female leadership and logged GDP, and the model explains very little of the variation in GDP. These results suggest that while GDP has generally increased over time, there is no consistent evidence from either the top-country analysis or the all countries with available gdp observations that female leadership alone is a strong predictor of economic outcomes.

Regime Type & Unemployment

Overall, the analysis shows that unemployment rates vary across political regime types, but the differences are not uniform or consistent across all measures. The summary table indicates that some regime types have higher average unemployment and a larger share of countries with high unemployment, while others tend to have lower levels. Over time, most regime types experienced increases in average unemployment, although the amount of change differs, with some regimes showing larger increases and others remaining relatively stable or even decreasing. The boxplot further shows substantial overlap in unemployment distributions across regimes, suggesting that no single regime type consistently has distinctly higher or lower unemployment. A closer look at royal dictatorships highlights that unemployment trends can fluctuate over time and vary widely across countries within the same regime. Overall, these results suggest that while unemployment differs by political regime type, there is no clear or consistent pattern indicating that one type of regime systematically leads to higher or lower unemployment outcomes.
